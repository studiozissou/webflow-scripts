# Sourcing Premium Vintage Menswear in the Netherlands

## Channel Analysis and Strategic Guide

**The Netherlands offers a deep but structurally imperfect market for sourcing heritage menswear.** A buyer based near The Hague can access roughly a dozen wholesale warehouses, hundreds of kringloopwinkels, monthly mega-flea-markets, and vigorous online platforms – yet the single greatest obstacle is consistent: the best pieces are systematically extracted before they reach you. Building direct relationships with sorting operations and wholesale owners, exploiting knowledge gaps around heritage brands that most Dutch resellers overlook, and timing purchases to seasonal rhythms are the three levers that separate productive sourcing from frustration. This report maps every viable channel, with realistic pricing, hit rates, and strategic assessments calibrated specifically for Burberry, Barbour, Harris Tweed, Shetland knitwear, Italian tailoring, quality leather, Japanese selvedge denim, and British contemporary heritage brands.

## 1. Wholesale handpick warehouses favour the knowledgeable buyer

The Dutch vintage wholesale sector has expanded rapidly since 2020, with most operations clustered in Noord-Holland and the central provinces. For a buyer targeting heritage menswear rather than the American sportswear brands (Nike, Ralph Lauren, Carhartt) that dominate this market, two warehouses stand apart.

**ROF Wholesale (Zaandam)** is the strongest option. Located 75 km from The Hague (~55 minutes by car), ROF explicitly stocks Barbour jackets, designer items from Missoni, Fendi, and Dolce & Gabbana, and maintains separate decade-sorted showrooms spanning the 1950s through 1980s. Their standard vintage rate is **€20/kg**; branded stock commands **€60/kg**. At that branded rate, a Barbour Beaufort (~1.2 kg) would cost roughly €72 – leaving viable margin for resale at €120–€180. Minimum purchase is 50 kg. Appointments available seven days a week. ROF also runs the **Vintage per Kilo** public events at €20/kg, which represent the best-value kilo sale for heritage fabrics given the pre-1980s stock dating window. ROF operates under the margeregeling and requires retail/business credentials, though enforcement appears flexible.

**Original Vintage Wholesale (Veldhoven, near Eindhoven)** offers the lowest handpick rate found at **€17.25/kg** (€14.75/kg for 25+ kg orders), with no minimum purchase and an A-grade-only policy. The founder's background in Italian antique trading means European-sourced stock with better odds of Italian wool blazers and quality leather. The 130 km distance from The Hague is the main drawback. Appointments required; no KVK registration demanded.

**The "cream" problem is real.** Industry insider Wildebras Vintage states bluntly: "The really good clothing is filtered out and sold to parties with much more money than the average vintage startup." At every wholesale operation, staff extract the highest-value items before stock reaches the customer floor. The countermeasure is relationship-building – particularly at smaller operations like Original Vintage and ROF where personal rapport with owners can unlock access to fresh stock drops.

**Japanese denim (Momotaro, Kapital, Studio D'Artisan) and niche British heritage (Nigel Cabourn, Margaret Howell) will not appear at any of these warehouses.** These specialist collector brands rarely enter the European wholesale chain. Source them online via Grailed, Marrkt, or Yahoo Japan Auctions.

## 2. Textile sorting mills offer the deepest wholesale access – if you can get in

The Netherlands processes approximately **234,000 tonnes of textiles annually** through industrial sorting operations. These mills represent the earliest point in the supply chain where individual garments are categorised – and where the most knowledgeable trade buyers extract premium pieces before they reach wholesale warehouses or kringloopwinkels.

**Midtex B.V. (Middenmeer, Noord-Holland)** is the key operation. Founded in 1994, Midtex imports textiles from Scandinavia, the USA, and across Europe, then micro-sorts into up to **300 distinct grade categories** – a single garment type such as men's t-shirts may be subdivided into 15 sub-grades by brand, style, and quality. Midtex maintains a select group of pickers who visit weekly by appointment to sort through categories including leathers, branded jeans, branded sportswear, and "hippie" mix. Access is by invitation and established trade relationship only. KVK registration is effectively mandatory. Pricing is negotiated per category and volume, but industry benchmarks suggest:

- **Unsorted mixed bales:** €0.50–€2.00/kg
- **Sorted rewearable A-grade:** €2.00–€5.00/kg
- **High-value "cream" or vintage-specific grades:** €10–€20+/kg
- **Wipers/rags:** €0.13–€0.45/kg

Other Dutch sorting operations include **Wieland Textiles** (Wormerveer, ~9 million kg/year, pioneer of automated Fibersort technology), **Ada Textile Recycling B.V.**, **Textile Recycling Dordrecht** (conveniently in Zuid-Holland), and **HOBA Holland** (IJmuiden, 6 million kg/year, also buys surplus stock and factory lots). All operate B2B and require trade credentials.

**Realistic assessment for heritage menswear:** A wool blazers or branded outerwear sort grade from Scandinavian or Northern European input streams would contain a meaningful proportion of quality tailoring. Building a trade relationship with a sorting mill is the single most scalable sourcing strategy – but the barrier to entry is high, requiring KVK registration, patience, and consistent purchasing volume to maintain the relationship.

## 3. Kringloopwinkels have risen in price but remain the highest-margin individual-piece source

Dutch charity shops have undergone a pricing revolution. Multiple chains now benchmark against Marktplaats and Vinted resale values, and internal cherry-picking of branded items is widespread. Customer reviews across Het Goed, RataPlan, and former Noppes locations consistently report that "all the gems are picked out in advance and sold at far too high prices" and that menswear sections are "dominated by C&A and Primark."

Despite this, kringloopwinkels remain the highest-margin channel for individual finds, because the arbitrage depends on knowledge gaps rather than volume.

**RataPlan Den Haag (Laan van Waalhaven 213a)** is the single best kringloopwinkel for a Hague-based buyer. At **5,050 m²**, it is officially the largest kringloopwinkel in the Netherlands, with an entire upper floor dedicated to clothing. Open seven days a week. The sheer volume means a higher absolute number of finds despite cherry-picking. RataPlan now operates 52 locations nationally after absorbing the Noppes chain.

**Leger des Heils / ReShare** operates 56 locations including a ReShare Store at Paviljoensgracht 35 in The Hague and a traditional kledingwinkel at Ambachtsgaarde 198. The ReShare format is modern and boutique-like, with clothing from **€3 per item**. Stock is washed and well-presented. Less aggressive on premium pricing than Het Goed.

**Het Goed** operates 30 locations but has no Hague branch (nearest: Rotterdam, ~25 km). It is consistently the most expensive chain – trousers at €20, vintage blouses at €17.50, branded items at €30–€105. Reviews describe prices as "above shop prices." **Not recommended for sourcing at resale margins.**

**Emmaus (Prinsegracht 36, The Hague)** offers the best chance of underpriced finds due to its chaotic, volunteer-run, unstructured inventory – but opens only Thursday, Friday, and Saturday (12:00–16:00). Prices are inconsistent and often unmarked.

**Schroeder Kringloop** has seven Hague locations. Less professionalised than the major chains, potentially meaning less aggressive cherry-picking. Worth regular visits to the Fahrenheitstraat and Torenstraat branches.

**Realistic hit rates for heritage menswear at kringloopwinkels:**

| Category | Hit rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Harris Tweed | 1 find per 10–15 visits | Dutch staff often don't recognise the label; may be priced at standard €8–€20 |
| Italian blazers (Canali, Corneliani) | 1–3 finds per 10 visits | Better represented in NL than British brands; look in affluent-area shops |
| Burberry | 1–2 finds per 20 visits | Immediately identified by staff; typically €25–€60 when found |
| Barbour | Near zero | Minimal Dutch cultural penetration; exceptionally rare |
| Japanese denim | Near zero | Niche even in fashion-forward markets |

**The competitive advantage for this buyer is brand knowledge.** Most Dutch resellers target Nike, Adidas, and Carhartt. A Harris Tweed jacket or Shetland knit lacking obvious brand recognition passes through staff filters unremarked.

## 4. Koningsdag is the single best sourcing day of the year

**King's Day (27 April)** transforms the entire Netherlands into a flea market. No permits are required; anyone can sell on the streets from 06:00 to 20:00. Amsterdam alone sees upwards of 3,500 sellers. The pricing dynamic is extraordinary: ordinary Dutch people clear wardrobes, attics, and deceased relatives' belongings at **€1–€15 per item**, with most sellers simply wanting things gone.

The Hague's own vrijmarkt is particularly promising. The city's international diplomatic community means a higher-than-average presence of British and Italian clothing in local wardrobes. **Target affluent residential streets in Archipelbuurt, Benoordenhout, and Statenkwartier** rather than main market squares. Amsterdam's Oud-Zuid (Beethovenstraat, Apollolaan) is the premium sourcing neighbourhood for those willing to travel.

**Estimated hit rate for premium heritage menswear: 15–25% chance of at least one significant piece per 3–4 hours.** This is the highest of any channel. Arrive by 06:00–07:00, bring small-denomination cash and a measuring tape, and set time limits per area.

**IJ-Hallen (NDSM wharf, Amsterdam)** runs monthly, with ~750 stalls. Entry is €6.50 (€11 for early bird access from 06:00). Travel from The Hague is roughly 75 minutes door-to-door. The market has become increasingly commercialised – more trade sellers with racks than private individuals – but the scale still generates finds. **Hit rate for premium heritage menswear: 5–10%.** The early bird ticket is essential; the best pieces vanish within two hours.

**Vintage per Kilo events** (run by ROF Wholesale) at **€20/kg** with 1940s–1980s stock are the best-value kilo events. A Harris Tweed jacket (~0.8 kg) costs roughly €16; a wool overcoat (~1.5 kg) around €30. Free entry, card payment only. Monitor their social media for Dutch dates.

**Vinokilo events**, by contrast, charge **€40–€55/kg** – making a wool blazer €28–€55 before you've even assessed quality. At these rates, the margin advantage over buying retail secondhand largely disappears. Stock comes from recycling warehouses and the premium heritage hit rate is very low (1–3%). **Not recommended for this buyer's target categories.**

Other markets worth noting: **Noordermarkt (Amsterdam, Saturday mornings)** for higher-end vintage (5–8% hit rate), **KiloStore Rotterdam (Korte Hoogstraat 10)** for convenient proximity at 25 km from The Hague, and **VerzamelJaarbeurs (Utrecht)** as a specialist collectors' fair with genuine potential for Nigel Cabourn, Barbour, and similar pieces.

## 5. Online platforms reward patience and niche knowledge

**Vinted** dominates Dutch C2C secondhand fashion after absorbing United Wardrobe in 2020. The platform charges zero seller fees (buyers pay a protection fee), making it both the primary buying and selling channel. For heritage menswear, the key insight is to **search brand names without selecting the brand filter** – this surfaces items from sellers who tagged similar brands in descriptions, revealing overlooked pieces from adjacent labels. Misspellings and generic titles ("vintage jacket," "tweed blazer") hide premium items from brand-aware competitors. Cross-domain searching (Vinted.nl, Vinted.co.uk, Vinted.fr, Vinted.it) dramatically expands the pool.

Typical Vinted buy prices for target brands:

- **Harris Tweed jacket:** €25–€80 (frequently underpriced)
- **Shetland knitwear:** €15–€40 (very undervalued)
- **Barbour waxed jacket:** €60–€150
- **Burberry vintage trench:** €80–€250
- **Momotaro/Japan Blue jeans:** €60–€150 (rare; often mispriced low by non-specialist sellers)
- **Sunspel/Margaret Howell:** €20–€60

**Marktplaats** is the strongest platform for bulk lots. Searching "partij vintage kleding" yields ~286 active listings at any time, with closing-shop stock at €1–€3/item in quantity and unsorted bulk at €0.50–€3/kg. Heritage menswear is embedded within mixed lots – budget for sorting time.

**Catawiki** runs weekly expert-curated vintage clothing auctions starting at €1, with a buyer's premium of 9% + €3. Quality is high but the format favours individual luxury pieces over bulk sourcing.

**Grailed** and **Marrkt** are the premier selling platforms for premium menswear. Grailed carries exactly the target brands (Kapital, Momotaro, Nigel Cabourn, Engineered Garments) with a knowledgeable buyer base. Marrkt specialises in pre-owned heritage menswear with curated price drops. Both are better as selling channels than sourcing – prices reflect informed market values.

## 6. Cross-border sourcing extends the catchment profitably

**Brussels – Place du Jeu de Balle** operates daily (06:00–14:00 weekdays, to 15:00 Sundays) with approximately 450 professional sellers. Sunday mornings yield the best stock. Prices run 10–25% below Dutch equivalents for unbranded goods. The Hague to Brussels is 175 km (~2 hours by car, ~1h50 by Thalys from €29). **An excellent monthly day-trip, combining the flea market with the Rue Blaes/Rue Haute antique corridor.** Hit rate for heritage menswear: 5–10%.

**UK bale buying** remains viable post-Brexit despite additional friction. Under the UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement, used clothing of UK origin qualifies for **0% tariff** with correct origin documentation. Dutch import VAT of 21% applies but is recoverable for VAT-registered businesses via an Article 23 permit. Key UK wholesale suppliers include **Worth The Weight** (West Midlands, from ~€1/kg for 400+ kg orders), **To Be Worn Again** (Sheffield, ~£80–£150 per 45 kg bale), and **Syed Vintage** (category-sorted branded bales). Total landed cost including shipping, customs clearance (~€30–€80/shipment), and VAT adds approximately **25–30%** to the purchase price – viable only for higher-value sorted bales, not generic mixed stock.

**German flea markets** are underexploited. Düsseldorf's **Trödel & Antikmarkt am Aachener Platz** (Saturdays from 06:00, 40+ years running) is 230 km from The Hague. The German Flohmarkt culture runs deeper than the Dutch equivalent, with more stock in circulation. Cash remains king.

**Instagram wholesale** has become a primary B2B channel. **SP Vintage Wholesale** (@spvintagewholesale, near Barcelona, 83,000+ followers) processes 200,000+ kg/year across 300+ categories. **Italian Vintage Wholesale** (italianvintagewholesale.com) ships across the EU with VAT exemption for B2B and specialises in Italian-sourced stock – the best option for Italian tailoring in bulk. **Fleek** (joinfleek.com) is a dedicated B2B marketplace connecting vintage retailers with 1,000+ verified suppliers, offering small MOQs of 10–20 pieces with buyer protection.

## 7. Estate sales and house clearances are a low-volume but high-potential channel

Clothing is almost never considered "auction-worthy" by Dutch auction houses, which focus on art, antiques, and design. Estate clothing typically flows to clearance companies or kringloopwinkels – creating an opportunity for a proactive buyer.

**The approach:** Contact Hague-based clearance companies – **Woningontruiming de Zwart** is the obvious local starting point – and offer to take clothing lots before they go to charity or disposal. Since clothing is a burden for most clearance firms (charities frequently reject large volumes), you may find willing sellers at **€0–€5 per bag**. The best prospects come from estates of older men from higher socioeconomic backgrounds – more likely to own quality tailoring, overcoats, and vintage accessories. Expect roughly 1 in 10–20 estates to yield meaningfully resalable menswear.

**BVA Auctions** (bva-auctions.com, merged with Troostwijk as TBAuctions) is Europe's largest online auction platform. Set alerts for clothing, fashion, and inboedel categories. **Vendu Rotterdam**, with 200+ years of operation and proximity to The Hague, specialises in complete household contents and is worth monitoring.

**Storage unit auctions barely exist in the Netherlands.** Only one clearly identified operator – Boxenveiling (Drachten, 220 km away) – conducts physical auctions. Dutch self-storage companies typically clear unpaid units privately. This is not a viable primary channel.

## 8. The legal framework rewards early registration

**The margeregeling (margin scheme)** is the critical tax mechanism for vintage resale. Instead of charging 21% VAT on the full selling price, you pay VAT only on your profit margin. Under the **globalisatiemethode** (the recommended calculation method), losses on one item offset gains on another within the same VAT return period. For a jacket bought from a private individual at €20 and sold at €120, VAT due is (€100 × 21/121) = **€17.36** rather than €24.79 under standard VAT. The scheme applies to goods purchased from private individuals, other margin-scheme dealers, or businesses that could not deduct input VAT. Invoices must state "Margeregeling" and may not show VAT separately. A purchase declaration (inkoopverklaring) is required for acquisitions of €500 or more.

**KVK registration as an eenmanszaak** costs a one-off **€82.25**, requires an appointment at any KVK office (bookable up to 8 weeks ahead via DigiD), and generates your BTW number within two weeks. Registration unlocks wholesale access, market permits, trade-buyer status, and the ability to claim the margeregeling. The **kleineondernemersregeling (KOR)** exempts businesses with turnover below €20,000/year from VAT entirely – but the threshold is based on full selling prices, not margins, and KOR prevents you from reclaiming input VAT on business expenses.

**Market stall selling** requires a marktvergunning from the relevant gemeente, with KVK registration as a prerequisite. The 14-day cooling-off period (bedenktijd) applies to all distance sales from a registered business. Accurately describing condition, noting defects, and stating "Margeregeling" on invoices are the essential compliance points.

## 9. Seasonal rhythms and realistic margins shape the business model

**The annual cycle is pronounced.** Outerwear (Burberry trenches, Barbour waxed jackets, wool overcoats, knitwear) commands a **30–50% price premium** when listed October–January versus spring/summer. The optimal strategy is to source heavily during the depressed-demand months of May–August and begin listing from late September.

Peak donation periods at kringloopwinkels – **January–February** (post-holiday decluttering), **March–April** (spring cleaning before King's Day), and **September** (wardrobe rotation) – are the windows for most productive charity shop visits.

Realistic resale prices in the current European market (sold prices, not listings):

| Item | Resale range | Key factors |
|---|---|---|
| Vintage Burberry trench (1960s–70s, Made in England) | €150–€350 | "Burberrys'" apostrophe branding commands premium |
| Barbour Beaufort/Bedale (vintage, good wax) | €80–€180 | Made in England labels worth 30–50% more |
| Harris Tweed blazer (generic) | €15–€40 | Named makers (J. Press, Dunn & Co) reach €30–€70 |
| Shetland/Fair Isle knitwear | €15–€60 | Premium labels (Jamieson's, Inverallan) top out at €80 |
| Italian blazers (Canali, Corneliani) | €40–€150 | Oversupplied; only unusual fabrics sell quickly |
| Momotaro jeans (good fade) | €80–€180 | Well-faded raw denim can appreciate; NWT holds 50–70% of retail |
| Kapital pieces | €60–€500+ | Patchwork/boro items command serious premiums |
| Nigel Cabourn mainline jackets | €150–€400 | Holds value well; Marrkt is the key platform |
| Margaret Howell outerwear | €80–€200 | MHL diffusion line 30–50% less |

**The highest-margin scenario** is a Barbour jacket found unrecognised at a kringloopwinkel for €8, sold on Vinted (zero seller fees) for €120 in season – a **€112 gross margin (93%)**. The lowest-margin scenario is generic wholesale at €15/kg resold at an average of €30/piece across 15 usable items from 20 kg – yielding a blended **8% gross margin** after platform fees and shipping. The lesson is stark: **targeted sourcing at charity shops and flea markets dramatically outperforms wholesale buying for heritage menswear specifically**, because the margin depends on knowledge-based arbitrage rather than volume.

## 10. Haarlem's curated vintage cluster: market intelligence and consignment potential

Haarlem (55 km from The Hague) has developed a notable concentration of high-end vintage shops, several of which are directly relevant to heritage menswear.

**Concrete Matter** operates in Haarlem alongside its Amsterdam flagship in the 9 Streets. It specialises in curated vintage workwear, denim, and military clothing – Pendleton, LL Bean, Filson, Woolrich, vintage selvedge denim, and leather flight jackets. It also carries Pike Brothers reproduction denim. This is the closest thing in the Netherlands to a shop aligned with the heritage menswear aesthetic, though prices reflect full retail curation.

**Paragons (Kleine Houtstraat 60)** stocks designer menswear sourced heavily from Italy – Jil Sander, Prada, Armani, Burberry – with prices from around €25. Roughly half the collection is menswear. Critically, Paragons accepts consignment of designer menswear, making it a potential selling channel.

**Nude Manifesto Studio** sells exclusively iconic vintage classics – Burberry trenchcoats, YSL smoking shirts, Christian Dior blazers – with private shopping appointments. Very much at the luxury end.

**Yenvi** combines new curated pieces online with premium vintage in their Haarlem shop, positioned around quality materials and lasting style.

**Episode** (Amsterdam-based chain) has a Haarlem branch with well-organised, category-sorted racks at lower price points than the boutiques above.

**Strategic assessment:** These shops are more useful as market intelligence than as buying sources. The prices at Paragons, Concrete Matter, and Nude Manifesto reflect full retail markup – you'd be paying what you could sell for, not sourcing inventory. However, they're excellent for calibrating what sells at what price point in the Dutch market, studying how dealers present and describe pieces, and building consignment relationships. Concrete Matter's brand selection confirms that heritage workwear/Americana has genuine demand in the Randstad. Combine a Haarlem visit with a trip to ROF Wholesale in Zaandam (another 20 minutes north) for a productive sourcing day.

## 11. Selling internationally to Japan and the USA

Both markets pay significantly more for heritage menswear than the Dutch domestic market, particularly Japan, where European vintage tailoring and British workwear command serious premiums.

### Platforms ranked for heritage menswear

**Grailed** is the primary channel for both markets. It has a global buyer base that actively searches for archive and heritage pieces. The "Sartorial" category and strong representation of Kapital, Visvim, Needles, and vintage designer pieces make it the natural home for this niche. Seller fees are 4.99% + $0.49 with no listing fees. As an international seller, you create your own shipping label and upload tracking. Duties and taxes are the buyer's responsibility.

**eBay** gives the broadest reach but requires more work. The Global Shipping Programme and eBay International Shipping are currently limited to UK- and US-based sellers respectively. Netherlands-based sellers must handle international shipping directly. eBay's auction format works well for genuinely rare pieces where competitive bidding pushes prices up.

**Etsy** has a predominantly US and Australian buyer base, with fees of $0.20 per listing plus 6.5% transaction fee. Better suited to items with a story – Harris Tweed, Shetland knitwear, heritage pieces that photograph and describe well with a lifestyle angle.

**Marrkt** specialises in exactly the target brands (Nigel Cabourn, Barbour, Margaret Howell) with an established buyer base willing to pay full value. Worth listing higher-end pieces here alongside Grailed.

**Yahoo Japan Auctions** (via Buyee or Zenmarket proxy) reaches the Japanese market directly but listing requires a Japan-based intermediary. Japanese buyers already shop on Grailed and eBay, making this unnecessary at early stage.

### Shipping from the Netherlands

**PostNL** is the default carrier. International tracked parcels start at approximately €13.00, with a maximum weight of 20 kg. For a single jacket or pair of jeans (1–2 kg), expect roughly €13–€18 tracked to the USA or Japan. Express service starts around €44. PostNL temporarily suspended US-bound parcels in late August 2025 after the abolition of the de minimis exemption but resumed shipping in September 2025 after new customs solutions were implemented.

**DHL and UPS** offer faster delivery (2–5 days vs 7–14 for PostNL) at higher cost (€25–€50 for a 2 kg parcel). Worth it for items above €150.

**Practical tip:** Vacuum-pack knitwear and lighter garments to reduce parcel size. A Shetland jumper compressed to letterbox-packet dimensions (under 3.2 cm thick, under 2 kg) can ship via PostNL brievenbuspakket internationally for around €4.65–€9.

### Tariffs and customs

**USA:** As of February 2026, a universal 10% Section 122 tariff applies to all imports, on top of existing baseline apparel duty rates of 12–15%. Combined, a buyer importing a €100 jacket could face 20–25% in duties and tariffs plus state sales tax. The Section 122 tariff is set to expire 24 July 2026 unless Congress extends it. These charges are the buyer's responsibility but affect their willingness to buy.

**Japan:** Import duty on used clothing under HS code 6309 is typically 0%. Consumption tax of 10% applies. Japan is significantly more favourable than the US – a €150 Barbour jacket costs a Japanese buyer perhaps €15–€20 in total import charges.

### International price premiums

| Item | Dutch domestic price | US/Grailed price | Japan price |
|---|---|---|---|
| Harris Tweed jacket | €15–€40 | $40–€80 | ¥8,000–25,000 (€50–€155) |
| Barbour waxed jacket (vintage, MiE) | €80–€180 | $150–€350 | ¥15,000–40,000 (€95–€250) |
| Shetland/Fair Isle knitwear | €15–€40 | $30–€60 | ¥5,000–15,000 (€30–€95) |
| Japanese denim (good fade) | €60–€120 | $100–€200 | ¥10,000–30,000 (€60–€185) |

### Practical strategy

Start by cross-listing best pieces on Grailed (US and Japan), Etsy (US and Australia), and Vinted (intra-European). Use Grailed for anything above €80, Etsy for €30–€100 items with a story, Vinted for everything else. Invest in good photography – flat-lay on neutral background, detail shots of labels, fabric texture, and wear. Include measurements in both inches and centimetres; Japanese buyers are extremely measurement-conscious. Always declare items as "used clothing" (HS code 6309.00) with accurate values.

## 12. Marrkt as a business model to emulate

Marrkt (marrkt.com) is the most instructive case study for a heritage menswear consignment platform – a bootstrapped, profitable business built on deep product knowledge rather than venture capital.

### Origin and history

Lewis Hull has been in premium menswear since 2008, initially as the UK distributor of Studio D'Artisan and later as the distributor and Covent Garden shop owner for The Real McCoy's. He also ran an online store called Superdenim. Marrkt launched in 2016, initially selling deadstock from brands like Nigel Cabourn – clearing their warehouses and sample stock. The pivot to pre-owned came in 2018, triggered by selling a personal Cabourn collection for a collector – a mix of pre-owned and new that sold out completely, confirming the market for pre-owned goods from well-made brands. After closing Superdenim and The Real McCoy's Covent Garden shop in 2019, Hull focused entirely on Marrkt. The business is now roughly 85% pre-owned, 15% surplus/deadstock.

### How the model works

The core is managed consignment for a curated niche, inspired by Japanese consignment stores in Tokyo and Osaka. Sellers send items to Marrkt's warehouse in Easingwold, outside York. Marrkt handles photography, listing, pricing, storage, customer service, and shipping.

**Commission structure (tiered):**

| Sale price | Marrkt commission |
|---|---|
| Up to £199 | 35% |
| £300–£499 | 30% |
| £500–£999 | 25% |
| Over £1,000 | 20% |

The business carries no inventory risk on consigned goods, and the sliding commission incentivises higher-value consignments.

### What makes it work

**Radical curation.** Items must be from brands they already carry and in at least good, clean condition. The brand list covers heritage menswear comprehensively: The Real McCoy's, Nigel Cabourn, Engineered Garments, RRL, Orslow, Kapital, Visvim, Drake's, Viberg, Tricker's, Beams Plus, Universal Works, Post Overalls, and Bryceland's.

**Content and community.** Featured Seller profiles turn anonymous transactions into editorial pieces about the people behind the wardrobes. Sellers have included Simon Crompton of Permanent Style and the architect Simon Allford (former RIBA president).

**Bootstrapped discipline.** Hull has stated: "There are some bigger players in the resale market who have received vast amounts of investment yet they can't make their models profitable. Marrkt is built on sound business foundations – we've had no external funding so we have to make a profit."

### Growth trajectory

- **2021:** Sold over 10,000 pre-owned items; sales more than doubled with a 300% increase in US sales.
- **2023:** 16,217 pre-owned items sold.
- **2024/25:** 20,000+ items annually from 2,000+ sellers worldwide.
- **Expansion into womenswear:** Margaret Howell, Comme des Garçons, APC, Kapital, Orslow, Story MFG, FWK Engineered Garments.
- **Own-brand production:** Made-for-Marrkt knitwear produced by Highland 2000.

### Price decay model

Marrkt operates a structured price reduction lifecycle visible in its site navigation: items progress through Price Drop 15% → 25% → 40% → Final Reduction, likely mapping to a 30/60/90/120-day cycle. This automated markdown cadence is a key feature to replicate.

### Implications for a continental European equivalent

There is no Marrkt-equivalent platform serving continental Europe. French, Dutch, German, and Scandinavian men who buy these brands currently sell through Vinted (underpriced, poor discovery), Grailed (US-centric, high shipping friction), or Marrkt itself (shipping to/from York adds cost and time). A managed consignment platform based in the Randstad, with shorter shipping loops to Paris, Brussels, Berlin, and Copenhagen, would have a structural advantage.

The realistic path: start by selling sourced items under a consistent brand identity on multiple platforms. Build a reputation and following. Once credibility and volume are established, begin accepting consignment from others in the Netherlands and surrounding countries. The transition from "curated reseller" to "managed marketplace" is the same one Hull made between 2016 and 2019.

## 13. The French-Japanese pipeline: an underexploited sourcing advantage

France has the deepest Japanese fashion connection of any European country, and understanding the supply chain behind this gives a Hague-based buyer a significant edge.

### Five pipelines feed Japanese brand supply in France

**1. Deepest retail stockist network in continental Europe.** Engineered Garments alone has six French stockists – in Annecy, Biarritz, Bordeaux, Marseille, Paris, and Toulouse – compared to zero in the Netherlands. Paris shops like The Next Door, Rendez-Vous, and Merci carry Orslow, Beams Plus, and Kapital as standard. Thousands of French consumers have bought these brands at retail for over a decade, and their wardrobes now cycle into the secondhand market.

**2. Gaijin Paris created a dedicated secondhand Japanese fashion market.** Founded by Thomas Robert and Chahine Bettat, Gaijin Paris started as a pop-up in the Marais and became a permanent shop at 20 rue du Pont aux Choux, offering curated secondhand Yohji Yamamoto, Comme des Garçons, Issey Miyake, Kapital, and others. This normalised the idea of buying and selling pre-owned Japanese designers among Parisian consumers.

**3. The Paris-Tokyo cultural axis.** Japanese designers showed in Paris from the early 1980s (the 1981 Comme des Garçons/Yohji Yamamoto debut). Paris has a large Japanese community, a Japanese quarter around Rue Sainte-Anne, and a fashion press that has covered Japanese brands seriously for 40 years. French menswear consumers have been exposed to these brands through French-language media and physical shops in a way that simply hasn't happened in the Netherlands.

**4. French resellers source directly from Japan.** The weaker yen (¥160–165 to the euro) has made buying from Japanese platforms extremely attractive. French Vinted sellers use Buyee (proxy service for Yahoo Japan Auctions and Mercari JP), FromJapan, and Zenmarket to buy pre-owned Kapital, Visvim, and Orslow at Japanese domestic prices – often 30–50% below European secondhand values even after shipping and import duty. A Kapital boro jacket at ¥15,000 (~€90) on Mercari JP can resell for €180–€300 on Vinted FR.

**5. Vinted FR is much larger than Vinted NL.** France is Vinted's biggest market by user count. More users means more listings, more niche subcultures, and enough specialist sellers to sustain dedicated Japanese brand accounts.

### Actionable implications

**Source from Vinted FR, not just Vinted NL.** Many French sellers don't realise their Japanese pieces sell for significantly more on Grailed or Marrkt. A Beams Plus jacket at €40–€60 on Vinted.fr can resell for $100–$150 on Grailed.

**Learn the Japan proxy buying route.** Buyee.jp and FromJapan.co.jp are straightforward: browse Yahoo Japan Auctions or Mercari JP, place bids or buy, and the proxy ships to the Netherlands. Budget roughly 10–15% in proxy fees plus €15–€30 shipping per parcel (sea mail cheaper but 4–6 weeks; airmail 7–14 days). Japanese import duty into the EU on clothing is typically 12% plus 21% Dutch BTW – but buying worn items at ¥5,000–¥15,000 means the total landed cost is still well below European resale value.

**Consider Paris as a sourcing destination.** A Thalys day trip from The Hague (under two hours) gives access to Saint-Ouen/Clignancourt flea market (far larger than anything in the Netherlands), Gaijin Paris, and the broader Marais vintage ecosystem. French stockists' end-of-season sales are another opportunity.

The core lesson: the Netherlands has virtually no infrastructure for Japanese heritage brands – no stockists, no cultural pipeline, no dedicated secondhand shops. France has all three. The competitive advantage is to exploit French and Japanese supply chains that most Dutch resellers don't know exist, and sell to the global market via Grailed, Marrkt, and your own platform.

## 14. Japan sourcing trips: the two-way arbitrage opportunity

A buying trip to Japan is one of the most productive sourcing moves available – and the reverse trade of importing European heritage brands to sell in Japan is equally viable. Japan's secondhand market operates at a quality standard dramatically higher than anything in Europe: items graded as "B condition" in Japan would typically pass as "excellent" on Vinted or Grailed.

### Where to buy in Japan

**Tokyo's key neighbourhoods for heritage menswear:**

**Shimokitazawa** has the broadest concentration of secondhand shops. KINJI offers prices generally between ¥2,000 and ¥5,000 (€12–€30), sometimes as low as a few hundred yen. New York Joe Exchange sells imported and domestic secondhand clothing with most items under ¥10,000 and 50% off on the first Sunday of each month.

**Koenji** (Suginami) is Tokyo's retro hub, packed with vintage shops. Safari is the best known for high-end menswear. More purist and less tourist-heavy than Shimokitazawa.

**Harajuku/Cat Street** is home to Ragtag (chain of designer-focused secondhand stores with separate menswear floors) and Rinkan (Shibuya flagship, specialising in designer clothing). Both carry Kapital, Engineered Garments, Visvim, and Comme des Garçons at 40–60% of European resale value.

**2nd Street** (chain with hundreds of locations nationwide) is the key to suburban bargain hunting. Stores outside central Tokyo (Kanagawa, Saitama, Chiba) are dramatically cheaper. Some locations run "nana fest" promotions on the 7th, 17th, and 27th of each month, offering 50% off. One buyer reported finding Alexander McQueen bootcut jeans for just ¥500 at a suburban location.

**Brand Collect** (Treasure Factory's luxury subsidiary) and **Kindal** are excellent for authenticated designer items at prices well below European equivalents.

**Wholesale warehouses** exist for registered dealers in the Tokyo suburbs and Kanagawa. Access requires proof of reselling activity (a Mercari account sales screen is accepted as evidence). Registration is required on first visit.

### Trip economics

A rough budget for a 7-day Tokyo sourcing trip:

| Cost | Estimate |
|---|---|
| Flights (AMS–NRT return) | €500–€900 |
| Accommodation (7 nights, business hotel) | €400–€560 |
| Local transport (rail pass + Suica) | €100–€150 |
| Food (€30–€50/day) | €210–€350 |
| **Total (excluding stock)** | **€1,200–€1,800** |

To justify the trip on sourcing economics alone, you need stock with a combined resale margin exceeding that figure. At an average buy price of €30–€50 per item and resale at €80–€200, roughly **20–30 pieces** break even on travel costs. Entirely achievable in 7 days of focused shopping.

### Getting stock home

**Carry it in luggage.** Most airlines allow 23 kg checked baggage (sometimes two bags on intercontinental routes). Clothing is light: 20–30 jackets, jeans, and knitwear items fit comfortably in two large suitcases. Buy an extra hard-shell case cheaply at Don Quijote if needed.

**Ship it.** Japan Post surface mail (SAL) costs roughly ¥4,000–¥6,000 for a 10 kg parcel to the Netherlands, taking 4–6 weeks. EMS (express) runs ¥7,000–¥12,000, arriving in 3–5 days. Post from any convenience store.

**Customs on return to the Netherlands:** Used clothing from Japan to the EU attracts approximately 12% duty (depending on fibre composition) plus 21% Dutch BTW on the CIF value. The EU traveller's allowance is €430 – anything above that is declarable. If registered for the margeregeling, BTW is recoverable against resale margin calculations.

### The reverse trade: European brands to Japan

Japan has a deep, almost reverential appreciation for European heritage brands. Shops like Hollywood Ranch Market run dedicated "Vintage Barbour" collections, re-proofing waxed jackets by washing and re-oiling – a level of curation showing how seriously the market takes these brands.

**What commands premiums in Japan:** Barbour, Harris Tweed, Burberry (especially vintage "Burberrys'" pieces), Shetland and Fair Isle knitwear, Grenfell, Mackintosh, Gloverall, Church's shoes. All sell at substantial premiums over European secondhand prices. Import duty on used clothing entering Japan is typically 0% under HS 6309, with only 10% consumption tax.

**The two-way arbitrage model:** Fly to Tokyo with a suitcase of European heritage menswear sourced cheaply from Dutch kringloopwinkels and UK bale buying. Sell or consign them to Japanese vintage shops at Japanese market prices. Use the proceeds to buy Japanese brands at domestic secondhand prices. Fly home and sell on Grailed and Marrkt at European/US market prices.

Example round-trip: A Barbour Beaufort bought at a Dutch kringloopwinkel for €12, sold to a Tokyo vintage shop for ¥25,000 (~€155). A Kapital Century Denim jacket bought at 2nd Street for ¥12,000 (~€75), sold on Grailed for $250 (~€230). The round-trip margin more than covers flights.

### Practical considerations

**Tax-free shopping (menzei)** is available to foreign tourists at many shops including 2nd Street and Ragtag for purchases over ¥5,000 at a single shop in one day. Saves 10% consumption tax. Bring your passport.

**Sizing:** Japanese sizing runs smaller than European. Heritage menswear (jackets, outerwear, knitwear) is less affected, but check measurements on tailored pieces.

**Timing:** January and July are sale seasons across Japan's retail sector, including secondhand shops. A January trip lets you source outerwear at discounted prices for the following European autumn/winter season.

**Language:** Most shops have prices clearly marked. Google Translate's camera function handles kanji on labels. English is limited but not a barrier to buying.

**Selling European stock in Japan:** The simplest route is listing on Grailed and eBay with international shipping. Japanese buyers actively shop both platforms. The more ambitious route is building relationships with Japanese vintage shops (Berberjin in Harajuku, Safari in Koenji, 2nd Street chain) that buy consignment stock from European suppliers. A sourcing trip doubles as a relationship-building opportunity.

## Conclusion: recommended sourcing strategy

The most productive strategy for a Hague-based buyer combines three tiers. **First tier (weekly):** regular early-morning visits to RataPlan Den Haag, Schroeder locations, and Emmaus, exploiting brand knowledge to spot Harris Tweed, Shetland knitwear, and Italian tailoring that passes through staff filters unremarked. **Second tier (monthly):** handpick sessions at ROF Wholesale's branded category, IJ-Hallen early bird visits, cross-domain Vinted searches (especially Vinted FR for Japanese brands), and Japan proxy buying via Buyee for targeted Kapital/Momotaro/Orslow acquisitions. **Third tier (quarterly/annual):** Koningsdag as the single highest-yield sourcing day, Brussels Jeu de Balle day-trips, Haarlem vintage cluster visits for market intelligence, VerzamelJaarbeurs Utrecht, UK bale buying for sorted branded stock, and an annual Japan sourcing trip combining inbound stock buying with outbound European brand sales.

For Japanese denim and brands like Nigel Cabourn or Margaret Howell, the online specialist platforms – Grailed, Marrkt, multi-country Vinted, Japan proxy services, and in-person Japan sourcing – are the only realistic channels; these items simply do not circulate through Dutch wholesale or charity infrastructure.

**Selling strategy:** List domestically on Vinted NL for faster-moving items under €80. Cross-list premium pieces on Grailed (US/Japan buyers), Etsy (US/Australia), and Marrkt (heritage menswear collectors). Target Japan as the primary international market due to 0% import duty and strong cultural demand for European heritage menswear; treat the US as secondary given current tariff uncertainty. Explore the two-way arbitrage model: source European heritage brands cheaply in the Netherlands, sell into Japan at premium prices, and use the proceeds to buy Japanese brands for resale in Europe and the US.

**Immediate next steps:** Register an eenmanszaak (€82.25, unlocks the margeregeling and wholesale access), begin with the globalisatiemethode for VAT, plan inventory purchases around the May–August sourcing window to sell October–January at peak outerwear demand, study the Marrkt model as a long-term template for building a continental European managed consignment platform, and begin planning a January Japan sourcing trip to coincide with sale season.
