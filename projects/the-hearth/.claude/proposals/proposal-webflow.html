Overview

Project context

The Hearth combines workspace, wellness, events, and community under one roof. Nobody else in London does all four. The current Squarespace site does not reflect that. It is too restrictive for the functionality required, too text heavy, and too many clicks stand between a visitor and a booking. The site needs to do more than describe The Hearth — it needs to convert visitors into members, space rental clients, and event attendees.

Problem

  • The current site converts at 2.8-4%. The target is 7%.
  • Space rental inquiries sit at 80-85/month against a target of 300.
  • The membership model has no platform to explain what it actually offers.
  • Booking flows push users through Eventbrite and multi-step forms. Too much friction.
  • The photography is 4-6 years old and does not look like the space does now.

Solution

  • Build a website on Webflow where every page is designed to convert.
  • Structure content around what each audience actually needs: space rental, co-working, wellness, community. Different people, different entry points.
  • Maximum two clicks from homepage to any inquiry form.
  • Set up a CMS so the team can publish events, blog posts, bios, and testimonials without a developer.
  • Wire up HubSpot, Mindbody, Stripe, GA4, and email marketing so bookings, payments, lead capture, and tracking all work without manual steps.
  • SEO, schema markup, and accessibility built in from day one.

What you get

  • A site that looks and feels like The Hearth actually is, not a generic co-working site, not a wellness studio site.
  • Membership pages that help women figure out which path fits them and book a trial day in two clicks.
  • A space rental landing page built to convert, with short forms feeding straight into HubSpot.
  • On-site event booking so you are not sending everyone to Eventbrite.
  • Content populated using copy provided by The Hearth team.
  • A CMS the team can manage independently. Events, blog, bios, testimonials, all editable.
  • Technical SEO, schema markup (LocalBusiness, Events, Organisation, Articles), and accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA) on every page.
  • Answer Engine Optimisation (AEO). Content structured so AI search tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) can find and cite The Hearth. Most agencies are not doing this yet.
  • GA4 setup with custom event tracking for form submissions, booking completions, CTA clicks, and newsletter signups.

Answers to your questions

1. Discovery — how do you approach understanding our positioning?

It starts with your brand guidelines, the messaging framework, and a conversation about what actually makes The Hearth different. I then look at what AllBright, The Wing, Trouble Club, Loom Club, and local co-working and venue hire options are doing — where the market is crowded and where the gaps are.

From there I map user journeys for each audience: space rental, co-working, social, wellness. How they arrive, what they need, where they drop off, what conversion looks like. The site architecture, wireframes, and content strategy come from that research.

The integrated model is the thing that sets you apart, so it needs to be visible in the site structure, not just the copy. Navigation, page hierarchy, and CTA placement all need to reflect the fact that someone might be here for workspace, wellness, community, or all three.

2. Platform — what do you recommend and why?

Webflow. Here is why:

  • The editor is straightforward for non-technical team members. Adding events, blog posts, bios, and testimonials is simple. No plugins to manage, no security patches, no database to maintain.
  • Webflow sites sit on a global CDN (Fastly/AWS). Page load times are fast without extra caching plugins or server config. That matters for your under 3 second target.
  • I get full control over layout and animation without the constraints of WordPress themes. The membership UX and the image heavy design direction both need that.
  • WordPress sites typically rely on 15-30 plugins for forms, SEO, caching, security, backups, and integrations. Each one is a potential point of failure and a maintenance cost. Webflow handles most of this natively.
  • Webflow handles SSL, hosting security, and backups. No WordPress core updates, no plugin vulnerabilities.
  • HubSpot, Stripe, and email marketing all integrate with Webflow via native integrations or lightweight custom code. Mindbody can be embedded as it currently is (pop-up panel) or integrated via their API. GA4 connects natively.

Trade-offs to be aware of:

  • Webflow does not have native user login or member portal functionality. If a member portal is needed in future, it would require a third party tool (e.g. Memberstack).

3. Integrations — do you have experience with what we need?

HubSpot CRM: yes. I have built HubSpot form integrations on multiple Webflow sites, including contact tracking and attribution (which form, which page, which campaign). All forms on the site will feed into HubSpot with automated workflows triggered by submission.

Mindbody: Mindbody offers embeddable widgets and an API. The current pop-up panel approach can be carried over and styled to match the new design, or we can look at a deeper integration if the booking flow needs to feel more native. I will spec the best approach during discovery.

Trial day booking: Stripe handles the payment. For calendar availability, the simplest approach is Mindbody (since you already use it for memberships) or a lightweight calendar embed (Calendly or Cal.com). I will spec the best option during discovery based on how trial days are currently managed.

Event booking: I recommend reducing Eventbrite dependency by building direct event booking into the site (Stripe for payment, Webflow CMS for event management). Eventbrite can stay as a secondary channel for discoverability, but the primary booking path should be on-site.

Email marketing: newsletter signup forms integrated with your chosen email platform (Mailchimp or an alternative).

GA4: full setup with custom events for form submissions, booking completions, CTA clicks, and newsletter signups. Approximately 3 hours of configuration.

4. Conversion optimisation — how do you approach improving conversion rates?

It starts in discovery. The journey mapping shows where people drop off and why. Then:

  • Maximum two clicks from homepage to any inquiry form. No unnecessary intermediate pages.
  • Never more than one scroll away from a relevant CTA. CTAs vary by user intent — not everything says "Book Now".
  • Short forms. Only ask for what you need. Name, email, event type, date, guest count for space rental. Nothing more.
  • Testimonials, member stories, and real quotes placed near conversion points.
  • 60%+ of traffic is mobile. Every form, button, and booking flow is designed for thumbs first.

Post-launch, GA4 and heatmap tracking (Hotjar or similar) show where users click, scroll, and drop off. The retainer packages include time for ongoing optimisation based on that data.

5. Training — how do you make sure the team can manage the site?

The goal is for the site to be mostly self explanatory after handover. That means:

  • Clearly labelled CMS collections, components, and fields with helptext where possible. The site should make sense without a manual.
  • A live training session (1-2 hours) to walk through the things that are not obvious — adding events, managing blog posts, updating bios, what to touch vs what not to touch.
  • Screen recordings of non-standard tasks for reference.

Webflow's editor is visual and designed for non-technical users. Your team will not need me for day to day content changes.

If you do get stuck, I am available ad hoc at £120/hour or through a retainer package.

6. Post-launch support — what is included after launch?

8 hours of post-launch support (30 days) included. Covers bug fixes, minor content tweaks, and troubleshooting. Same business day response for anything critical.

After that:

  • Retainer packages available (see Monthly Retainer section).
  • Ad hoc support at £120/hour.
  • If the site goes down or an integration breaks, that is always priority regardless of support arrangement.

7. Membership presentation

This is a UX challenge, not just a content challenge. Traditional tier comparison tables do not work here because the value is not "more desk hours = higher tier". The value is "we meet you where you are".

My recommended approach:

  • A short interactive element (not a quiz, more like a guided question) that helps women identify which journey fits: work, wellness, social, or integration. This leads them to the most relevant membership information.
  • Each journey gets its own content section explaining what support looks like (space, community, resources, programming). Pricing comes after the value is clear, not before.
  • The design needs to communicate that memberships bend to real life. Visual and copy cues that say "your membership shifts as your needs shift", not rigid categories.
  • Member testimonials organised by journey type. A woman on a wellness journey sees a testimonial from someone who came for the same reason.

I have not designed this exact model before, but I have designed multi-audience sites where each audience needs a different entry point and a different value proposition (Temper, Carsa). The UX principles are the same: help someone figure out where they fit, show them what is relevant, and get rid of everything that is not.

Future enhancements

Things that are not in this proposal but would build on what we launch:

  • A/B testing on the space rental page. Test headlines, CTAs, form length, and imagery to push the inquiry rate past 7%. Can run via Webflow Optimize or Google Optimize.
  • Direct membership purchase on-site. Once the journey-based model is proven, add Stripe checkout so women can buy memberships without going through Mindbody.
  • Member portal. A logged-in area for members to manage bookings, view upcoming events, and access member-only content. Would require Memberstack or similar.
  • Automated review collection. Post-event and post-trial-day emails that prompt Google reviews and testimonials. Feeds the social proof loop and boosts local SEO.
  • Neighbourhood landing pages. SEO play for "women's co-working Queen's Park", "event space NW6", etc. Each page targets a specific local search intent.
  • Video integration. If The Hearth produces video content (member stories, space walkthroughs, event highlights), embed natively with schema markup for video search visibility.

Any of these can be scoped as retainer work or a separate project.
