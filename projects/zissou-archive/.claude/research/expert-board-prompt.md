## Local Market Intelligence (all advisors have read this briefing)

The board has been briefed on the following market conditions specific to Den Haag and the Netherlands. They should reference these facts where relevant rather than speculating.

- **Store du Nord** (Noordeinde 49, Den Haag) is the lone premium heritage menswear specialist in the city. Owner Jeroen de Wal (25+ yrs in trade) stocks Barbour, Nigel Cabourn, Japan Blue, Momotaro, Harley of Scotland. His webshop reportedly went offline May 2026 — leaving an online gap in the exact niche Zissou Archive targets.
- **Den Haag has dozens of vintage/second-hand shops but almost none in premium heritage menswear.** The Dutch premium-menswear community is small, denim-led, and clustered in Amsterdam/Haarlem/Arnhem.
- **Key NL benchmarks:** Concrete Matter (Amsterdam, ~27k IG, military/workwear curation — skews workwear, leaves space for tailoring/British-Italian angle), Besnard (Haarlem, the Dutch Permanent Style anchor — sells new RTW, not pre-owned, so a collaborator not a competitor), Neighbourhood/Quality Goods (Arnhem, one of few NL Drake's stockists).
- **Modefabriek (main NL fashion trade fair) went womenswear-only from FW24** — not a menswear sourcing or networking route.
- **Sourcing — wholesale:** ROF Wholesale (Zaandam, 75 km) is the strongest option at €20/kg standard, €60/kg branded — a Barbour Beaufort (~1.2 kg) costs ~€72, resale €120-180. Minimum 50 kg. Original Vintage Wholesale (Veldhoven, 130 km) offers €14.75-17.25/kg hand-pick, no minimum, A-grade — best fit for curated heritage. Brasco (Amersfoort, 90 km) sells 20 kg bags or 100 kg bulk bags (no handpick, pre-packed) with stock spanning 70s-2000s sourced from Europe and USA — browsable clothing mixes at brasco.nl/category/clothing/mixes/ make it more accessible than pure bale buying; 20 kg is a viable test order. Blind bale wholesale generally skews off-niche. The "cream problem" is real: staff extract the best items before stock reaches the customer floor. Relationship-building with owners is the countermeasure.
- **Sourcing — kringloopwinkels:** RataPlan Den Haag (Laan van Waalhaven) is the largest kringloopwinkel in NL at 5,050 m². Emmaus (Prinsegracht 36) is chaotic and volunteer-run — best chance of underpriced finds but only open Thu-Sat 12:00-16:00. Schroeder Kringloop has 7 Den Haag locations, less professionalised = less cherry-picking. **Heritage menswear hit rates:** Harris Tweed 1 in 10-15 visits, Italian blazers 1-3 per 10 visits, Burberry 1-2 per 20 visits, Barbour near zero (minimal Dutch cultural penetration). **The competitive advantage is brand knowledge** — most Dutch resellers target Nike/Adidas/Carhartt; a Harris Tweed jacket passes through staff filters unremarked.
- **Sourcing — events:** Koningsdag (27 April) is the single best sourcing day of the year — no permits, €1-15/piece, 15-25% hit rate for heritage menswear. Target affluent streets in Archipelbuurt, Benoordenhout, Statenkwartier. The diplomatic community means higher-than-average British/Italian clothing in local wardrobes. IJ-Hallen monthly at NDSM (€6.50 entry, €11 early bird — essential, best pieces gone in 2 hours), stall €36-42/day. ROF's Vintage per Kilo events at €20/kg with 1940s-80s stock are the best-value kilo events. Vinokilo at €40-55/kg is NOT recommended — margin advantage disappears.
- **Sourcing — online:** Vinted (zero seller fees) dominates Dutch C2C. Key tactic: search brand names WITHOUT the brand filter to surface mislabelled items. Cross-domain searching (Vinted.nl/.co.uk/.fr/.it) dramatically expands the pool. **Vinted FR is much larger than Vinted NL** — French sellers often don't realise their Japanese pieces sell for significantly more on Grailed/Marrkt (a Beams Plus jacket at €40-60 on Vinted.fr resells for $100-150 on Grailed).
- **Sourcing — France pipeline:** France has the deepest Japanese fashion infrastructure in Europe — Engineered Garments alone has 6 French stockists vs zero in NL. Gaijin Paris (20 rue du Pont aux Choux) is a dedicated secondhand Japanese fashion shop. Paris is under 2 hours by Thalys. Saint-Ouen flea market is far larger than anything in NL.
- **Japan trade:** Hollywood Ranch Market re-proofs vintage Barbour for the Japanese market. Buyee (~¥500/order) and ZenMarket (~¥300/item, 45-60 day storage) are the proxy services for sourcing Japanese brands. Japan import duty on used clothing under HS 6309 is typically 0% + 10% consumption tax — far more favourable than the US (current 10% Section 122 tariff + 12-15% baseline apparel duty). **Two-way arbitrage model:** fly to Tokyo with European heritage sourced cheaply from NL kringloopwinkels, sell at Japanese prices, buy Japanese brands at domestic secondhand prices, sell on Grailed/Marrkt at EU/US prices. A 7-day Tokyo trip costs ~€1,200-1,800 excluding stock; 20-30 pieces break even.
- **Japan sourcing:** Best shops: 2nd Street chain (suburban locations cheapest, "nana fest" 50% off on 7th/17th/27th), Ragtag and Rinkan (40-60% of EU resale), Shimokitazawa (KINJI at ¥2,000-5,000). January and July are sale seasons. Tax-free shopping for foreign tourists on purchases over ¥5,000.
- **Selling platforms:** Grailed: 4.99% + $0.49 seller fee (no listing fee). Marrkt consignment: tiered 20-35% commission (up to £199 = 35%, £300-499 = 30%, £500-999 = 25%, £1,000+ = 20%). Etsy: $0.20/listing + 6.5% transaction fee. Vinted: zero seller fees. **Recommended strategy:** Vinted NL for items under €80, Grailed for premium pieces (US/Japan buyers), Etsy for story-driven items €30-100 (US/Australia), Marrkt for heritage collector pieces.
- **Marrkt business model details:** ~85% pre-owned, 15% surplus/deadstock. Automated price decay lifecycle: 15% → 25% → 40% → Final Reduction (likely 30/60/90/120-day cycle). Sold 20,000+ items annually from 2,000+ sellers worldwide by 2024/25. No external funding — bootstrapped and profitable. Now expanding into womenswear and own-brand production (knitwear by Highland 2000). **There is no Marrkt-equivalent platform serving continental Europe** — this is the long-term strategic opportunity.
- **Seasonal rhythms:** Outerwear commands 30-50% price premium listed Oct-Jan vs spring/summer. Optimal strategy: source heavily May-Aug, list from late September. Peak kringloopwinkel donation periods: Jan-Feb (post-holiday), Mar-Apr (pre-Koningsdag), September (wardrobe rotation).
- **Resale benchmarks:** Vintage Burberry trench (1960s-70s, MiE): €150-350. Barbour Beaufort (vintage, good wax): €80-180. Harris Tweed blazer: €15-40 (named makers €30-70). Momotaro jeans (good fade): €80-180. Kapital: €60-500+. Nigel Cabourn mainline: €150-400. Highest-margin scenario: Barbour found at kringloopwinkel for €8, sold on Vinted for €120 = €112 gross margin (93%).
- **Barbour rewax:** Official: £35 (rewax), £50 (clean + rewax). Independent rewaxers: Oily Jack (Kent), Mullaney Brothers (~10-week turnaround), WAXXED.
- **Legal/tax:** KVK registration as eenmanszaak: €82.25. Margeregeling (margin scheme): VAT only on profit margin — globalisatiemethode lets losses offset gains within a period. Kleineondernemersregeling (KOR): VAT exempt below €20k turnover but based on full selling prices not margins, and prevents reclaiming input VAT.
- **Local infrastructure:** Hakky Vakschoenmakerij (Wagenstraat 2A) has Goodyear AND Blake stitching machines. Haagsche Tailor (Gedempte Gracht 3A) does alterations and delicate-fabric cleaning.
- **KABK Textile & Fashion** (Den Haag): student photographers/stylists available for paid or TFP collaborations.
- **COSH!** (cosh.eco): indexes Den Haag ethical stores — free listing for circular credibility.

- **Estate clearances:** Contact Hague-based clearance companies (e.g. Woningontruiming de Zwart) and offer to take clothing lots before charity/disposal — clothing is a burden for most firms, €0-5 per bag. Best prospects: estates of older men from higher socioeconomic backgrounds. ~1 in 10-20 estates yields meaningfully resalable menswear.
- **Cross-border:** Brussels Place du Jeu de Balle (daily, 450 sellers, 175 km/~2 hrs by car) — prices 10-25% below NL equivalents. Düsseldorf Trödel & Antikmarkt am Aachener Platz (Saturdays, 230 km) — deeper Flohmarkt culture than NL.

## How the session works

1. **I'll share my business plan** with you after this prompt.
2. **Each advisor reads the full plan** and prepares their critique.
3. **Round 1 — Initial Reactions:** Each advisor gives their top-line take (2-3 sentences) and their single biggest concern about the plan. Go in order: Simon, Keiko, Pieter, Marieke, Tomás, Lewis.
4. **Round 2 — Deep Critique:** Each advisor picks the 2-3 sections most relevant to their expertise and gives detailed feedback: what's good, what's missing, what's wrong, and what they'd do differently. They should reference specific sections and numbers from the plan.
5. **Round 3 — Cross-examination:** The advisors challenge each other's feedback where they disagree. Simon might think TikTok is a mistake; Tomás might argue it's essential. Pieter might think the financial projections are fantasy; Marieke might defend them based on her experience. Lewis might argue the whole reselling model is wrong — he made that exact pivot in 2018 and it changed everything. Let the disagreements play out naturally.
6. **Round 4 — Action Items:** Each advisor gives me their top 3 things I should do in the next 30 days, prioritised. Then the board collectively agrees on a "consensus top 5" that they all support despite their different perspectives.

## Rules

- Stay in character throughout. Each advisor should have a consistent voice and perspective.
- Be specific — reference section numbers, financial figures, and concrete details from the plan.
- Don't be nice for the sake of it. I want honest, critical feedback that makes the plan better.
- If an advisor thinks part of the plan is genuinely good, they should say so — but briefly. Spend more time on what needs fixing.
- When advisors disagree, let the argument develop. Don't resolve it artificially — present both sides and let me decide.
