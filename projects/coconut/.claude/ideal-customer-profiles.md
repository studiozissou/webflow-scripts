# Coconut — Ideal Customer Profiles

**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Source:** Copy analysis across homepage, about, features, MTD landing page, pricing page.

---

## ICP 1: The Overwhelmed Sole Trader

### Role / Demographic
UK sole trader running a small trade, service, or creative business. Likely 1-5 years into self-employment. Could be a plumber, graphic designer, personal trainer, tutor, or similar. Income range: 20k-80k. Works alone or with occasional subcontractors.

### Goals
- Spend less time on admin and more time on billable work
- Know how much tax they owe before it's due
- File Self Assessment or MTD returns without hiring an accountant
- Feel confident their finances are in order

### Pain Points
- Tax feels stressful and confusing — they don't have an accounting background
- Receipts pile up in shoeboxes, wallets, and email inboxes
- End-of-year tax return is a dreaded annual scramble
- They don't know what expenses they can claim
- Spreadsheets are tedious and error-prone
- They worry about getting something wrong with HMRC
- MTD is approaching and they don't understand what's changing

### Language They Use
- "I hate doing my taxes"
- "I just want to focus on my business"
- "I don't know what I can claim"
- "How much tax do I owe?"
- "I need something simple"
- "Self Assessment", "tax return", "expenses"
- Search terms: "self employed accounting app", "how to do self assessment", "what expenses can I claim as sole trader"

### What They Need From the Site
- Immediate reassurance that this is for people like them (not accountants, not limited companies)
- Clear pricing with no hidden costs
- Evidence it's genuinely simple (not "simple" in the way enterprise software says "simple")
- A risk-free way to try it (free trial, no card)
- Plain-English explanation of MTD and whether it affects them
- Social proof from other sole traders

### Coconut Plan Fit
Bookkeeping (Essentials) or +Self Assessment, depending on whether they file SA themselves.

## ICP 2: The MTD-Anxious Earner

### Role / Demographic
UK sole trader or landlord earning above 50k (immediate mandate) or 30k-50k (April 2027 mandate). May already use spreadsheets or basic bookkeeping. Could be a landlord with rental properties, a CIS subcontractor, or an established tradesperson. Often older, less tech-confident.

### Goals
- Comply with Making Tax Digital without getting fined
- Understand exactly what MTD requires of them
- Find HMRC-recognised software they can trust
- Keep doing what they're doing with minimal disruption

### Pain Points
- Confused by what MTD actually means in practice
- Worried about quarterly reporting — feels like more work
- Uncertain whether their current tools (spreadsheets, accountant, paper) will still work
- Fear of HMRC penalties for non-compliance
- Don't want to learn complicated software
- May feel resentful that HMRC is forcing a change

### Language They Use
- "Making Tax Digital", "MTD", "quarterly updates"
- "Do I have to do this?"
- "What happens if I don't comply?"
- "HMRC recognised software"
- "Is this compatible?"
- Search terms: "MTD for income tax software", "do I need MTD", "MTD penalties", "free MTD software", "MTD compatible software HMRC"

### What They Need From the Site
- Clear timeline: when MTD affects them based on their income
- Confirmation that Coconut is HMRC-recognised (trust signal)
- Step-by-step explanation of what changes (quarterly updates, EOPS, Final Declaration)
- Reassurance that the transition is painless
- Human support availability — not just chatbots
- FAQ answers to specific compliance questions

### Coconut Plan Fit
MTD Filing or Full (if they also need Self Assessment).

## ICP 3: The Accountant Managing Sole-Trader Clients

### Role / Demographic
UK accountant or bookkeeper with a portfolio of sole-trader and landlord clients. Likely a small practice (1-10 staff). Already uses professional accounting software for limited companies but needs an efficient workflow for sole-trader MTD obligations.

### Goals
- Manage multiple clients' MTD submissions from one place
- Reduce time spent chasing clients for receipts and bank statements
- Offer MTD-readiness as a service to retain clients
- Maintain visibility over client finances in real-time

### Pain Points
- Clients are disorganised — late receipts, missing records, incomplete data
- The volume of sole-trader clients makes individual hand-holding impractical
- Existing enterprise tools (Xero, QuickBooks) are overkill or overpriced for sole traders
- They need to justify the cost and effort of switching tools
- MTD is creating urgent demand from clients who need help

### Language They Use
- "Client portal", "accountant portal"
- "Bank statements", "digital records"
- "MTD submissions", "quarterly updates"
- "Practice management"
- Search terms: "MTD software for accountants", "sole trader accounting software accountant portal", "coconut accountant login"

### What They Need From the Site
- A dedicated accountant-facing page with clear value proposition
- Real-time client data sharing without manual exports
- Bulk or multi-client pricing clarity
- Integration details — what banks, what data formats
- Confidence that the product is robust enough for professional use while being simple enough that clients will actually use it

### Coconut Plan Fit
Accountant product (separate from consumer plans).

## Cross-Cutting Observations

**What all ICPs share:**
- Low accounting literacy (or low patience for accounting complexity)
- UK-specific tax obligations (SA, MTD, HMRC)
- Price sensitivity — these are small operators, not funded startups
- Trust concerns around financial data security
- Mobile-first or mobile-friendly expectations

**Gaps in the current copy:**
- ICP 1 is well-served. The homepage and features pages speak directly to them.
- ICP 2 is well-served by the MTD landing page, though the volume of FAQ content could be better structured.
- ICP 3 is underserved on the audited pages. The accountant audience gets a nav link and brief mentions but no substantive pitch on main pages.
- No copy addresses landlords as a distinct segment. They're always mentioned alongside sole traders but never given their own messaging or use cases.
