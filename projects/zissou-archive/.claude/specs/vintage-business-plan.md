## 1. Key Assumptions

These are the assumptions the entire plan rests on. If any prove false, revisit the dependent sections.

### Market assumptions

1. **Demand for curated vintage menswear exists online and is growing.** Evidence: Vinted's 75M+ users, Grailed's dedicated menswear market, the success of specialist sellers like VintageFolk and Messina Hembry. The broader second-hand clothing market grew ~15% YoY in 2024-2025.

2. **The 3-5x margin on heritage brands is sustainable.** Evidence: your own proven flips (Zegna 5x, Crombie 5x, Grenfell 4x, Gimo's 3x). This depends on continuing to find underpriced items from sellers with bad photos or missing brand info. As reselling gets more popular, this arbitrage may narrow — but niche brand knowledge (knowing Pal Zileri from Primark) is a durable edge.

3. **There is no serious competitor in NL doing curated Permanent Style-adjacent vintage menswear online.** Evidence: store research confirmed no NL shop occupies this niche. Episode and Kilo Kilo are volume/unisex. Ilka and Evy's are women's luxury. Store du Nord does new Japanese denim, not vintage tailoring. This gap may not last forever.

4. **International buyers (Japan, US) will pay premium + shipping for the right items.** Evidence: Hollywood Ranch Market has traded vintage Barbour for decades. Japanese-to-US resale transactions grew 60% YoY in 2024. Grailed's audience is heavily US/Japan. But you haven't tested this yet — it's an assumption until your first international sale.

5. **Long-tail SEO can drive direct sales from a personal website.** Evidence: transactional searches like "vintage Barbour Bedale C40" exist and are underserved by niche sites. But SEO takes 6-12 months to compound, and you're competing with eBay/Etsy/Grailed in search results. Your web expertise gives you an edge, but traffic volume will be modest in year 1.

### Operational assumptions

6. **1 day/week is enough to build meaningful momentum.** This is ~4-5 hours of productive work per week after you subtract sourcing travel time. Realistic output: photograph 5-8 items, list 5-8 items, pack/ship 2-3 orders, 1 social media post. Scaling to 2 days/week doubles this.

7. **Your existing Zissou Archive automation saves meaningful time.** The Claude Vision scanning + Notion pipeline eliminates ~15-20 minutes per listing (writing descriptions, pricing research). At 10 items/week, that's 2.5-3 hours saved. This is a real competitive advantage.

8. **Home storage is viable up to ~50-75 items.** After that, you need dedicated space. A 5m² unit at ~€100/month is the likely next step.

9. **The margeregeling (margin scheme) applies to most purchases.** True for items bought from private sellers on Vinted, Marktplaats, flea markets, and charity shops. If you start buying from VAT-registered businesses (wholesale, new stock from Japan), different rules apply.

### Financial assumptions

10. **Average profit per item will increase over time (€20 → €35-45).** This assumes you move toward higher-value items (Barbour flips, Italian tailoring, Stone Island) and international sales as you build expertise and platform reputation. If you stay at the €20-margin-per-item level, the conservative scenario (~€3,200/year) is the ceiling.

11. **Platform fees stay roughly where they are.** Vinted's zero-seller-fee model is their key differentiator and likely stable. Grailed at ~13% and eBay at ~13% could change. A fee increase on any platform directly hits margins.

12. **You can source enough quality stock at 1 day/week.** This is the tightest bottleneck. Kringloopwinkels require physical visits. Vinted sourcing can be done in spare moments (phone). eBay auctions require attention. If sourcing dries up, everything stalls.

### What would invalidate the plan

- If margins compress to <2x consistently (market gets too competitive)
- If you can't find 5+ quality items per week to source
- If no international buyer materialises after 6 months on Grailed/eBay
- If the time commitment causes burnout alongside dev work
- If a well-funded competitor enters the NL curated vintage menswear space

## 3. Vision & Positioning

**One-liner:** Permanent Style quality at accessible prices, sourced sustainably.

Zissou Archive is a curated vintage and pre-owned menswear shop focused on heritage quality — Italian and British tailoring, natural fabrics, timeless silhouettes. Not stuffy, not streetwear. Think: the wardrobe of a well-dressed man who buys once, buys well, and keeps things for decades.

**The gap:** Nobody in the Netherlands is doing curated, Permanent Style-adjacent vintage menswear online with proper storytelling, measurements, and international reach. Vintage shops in NL are either kilo-sale bulk, women's-focused, or high-end designer consignment. The intersection of "heritage menswear + affordable + sustainable + well-presented" is empty.

**Brand voice:** Quiet authority. Simon Crompton's Permanent Style is the north star — knowledgeable, never flashy, trusted. A seller who knows what a half-canvas jacket feels like and can explain why it matters.

**Target customer profiles:**
- **Primary:** Men 28-55 who read Permanent Style, Styleforum, Put This On but can't afford new Isaia or Barbour. They know what they want but need someone to find it for them.
- **Secondary:** Japanese and American heritage menswear enthusiasts searching for specific items (vintage Barbour, Stone Island, military surplus) — willing to pay premium + international shipping.
- **Tertiary:** Style-curious men who want to dress better but don't know where to start. Reached via content/YouTube.

## 6. Sourcing Strategy

### Current approach (from vinted-brands.json)

**Core method:** Heritage/luxury menswear brands with high RRP, bought cheap from sellers with bad photos and missing brand info. Margin target: 3-5x.

### Sourcing channels (ranked by ROI for startup phase)

**1. Vinted (primary — current)**
- Pro: Zero fees, huge inventory, algorithm rewards active sellers
- Strategy: Search by brand, filter by size, sort by newest. Look for listings with terrible photos, wrong brand names, missing size info. These are underpriced.
- Your existing brand list (47 confirmed + needed) is strong. Keep expanding.
- Use V-Hive extension for quick pricing decisions.

**2. Kringloopwinkels (charity/thrift shops — NL)**
- Leger des Heils, Emmaus, Het Goed, local kringloops
- Pro: Lowest buy prices (€2-15 per item). Volume sourcing.
- Con: Time-intensive. Hit rate is low (~1 good item per 10 visited).
- Strategy: Build a rotation of 5-6 kringloops within driving distance of Den Haag. Visit each every 2-3 weeks. Build relationships with staff — ask them to hold heritage menswear aside for you.
- Best kringloops near DH: RataPlan (Voorburg), Kringloopwinkel Nootdorp, Het Goed (Delft), Emmaus (Den Haag)

**3. Marktplaats / Facebook Marketplace (NL)**
- Pro: Local collection, negotiation possible, sometimes bulk lots
- Strategy: Set up saved searches for key brands. Respond fast to new listings.

**4. eBay (international sourcing)**
- Pro: Global reach, auction format means bargains, especially from UK sellers who don't know European demand
- Strategy: Search for misspelled brand names (e.g. "Barbor", "Zegner"), ending-soonest auctions with few bids, and bulk lots.
- Japan proxy buying via Buyee (buyee.jp) for Japanese brands at domestic prices

**5. IJ-Hallen and flea markets (bulk sourcing events)**
- Pro: High volume, negotiation, instant assessment of items
- Con: Travel time, early wake-ups, competition from other resellers
- Strategy: Go with a clear hit list. Don't browse aimlessly.

**6. Estate sales and house clearances**
- Pro: Highest quality items, often priced for quick sale
- Con: Infrequent, hard to find
- Strategy: Check Notarishuizen.nl, Catawiki lots, and local funeral/clearance companies

**7. End-of-season sales (new stock for resale)**
- Pro: Legitimate new items at steep discounts
- Strategy: Outlet stores (Lelystad Fashion Outlet, Bataviastad) for heritage brands at 50-70% off RRP

### Value-add flipping strategies

**Barbour rewaxing:**
- Buy tired/dirty Barbour for €10-20
- Send to UK rewaxing service (WAXXED, Oily Jack): ~£25-35
- Total cost: ~€50-60
- Sell as "professionally rewaxed" for €120-200
- Margin: 2-4x on a desirable item
- Target market: Japan (Hollywood Ranch Market has traded vintage Barbour for decades), US heritage menswear community

**Other value-adds to consider:**
- Professional dry cleaning of wool coats/suits before listing
- Replacing missing buttons with period-appropriate ones (horn, corozo)
- Minor tailoring repairs (reattaching lining, fixing hems)
- Having shoes resoled (cobbler cost ~€30-50, adds €50-100 in value)

## 9. New Items — Japanese Brands Gap

### The gap is real

The Netherlands has **no physical specialist retailer** stocking Japanese heritage menswear brands. Available in NL:
- **Uniqlo** (Amsterdam, Den Haag) — mass market only
- **Store Du Nord** (Den Haag) — Japanese raw denim (Fullcount, Big John, Japan Blue) but not broader RTW
- **MTKN** (Amsterdam) — denim community platform, not a shop

**Not available in NL (no stockist):**
- Kamakura Shirts
- Kapital
- Beams Plus
- United Arrows
- Visvim
- Buzz Rickson's / The Real McCoy's
- Whitesville, Loop & Weft, The Flat Head (t-shirts)

### Could you sell new Japanese items?

**Opportunity:** Import and resell Japanese-made heavyweight t-shirts (Whitesville, Loop & Weft, The Flat Head) — items that cost ¥5,000-8,000 in Japan (~€30-50) and have no European distribution.

**Challenges:**
- Import VAT (21%) + customs duties on textiles (~12%) increase landed cost by ~33%
- A ¥6,000 t-shirt + ¥1,500 shipping = ~€50 landed → +33% tax = ~€67 cost → need to sell at €90+ for viable margin
- MOQ (minimum order quantities) from brands may be prohibitive for a solo operator
- **Proxy buying** (via Buyee or ZenMarket) adds fees on top

**Realistic approach:**
- Start by sourcing a small batch (5-10 units) via Buyee to test demand
- Position as "the only place in NL to buy genuine Japanese heavyweight tees"
- Partner with Store Du Nord or approach MTKN for collaboration
- If demand proves out, approach brands directly for wholesale terms (attend Modefabriek with samples)

**Risk:** Don't over-invest in new stock. The margin on new Japanese items is tighter than vintage. This should be a complement to the vintage core, not a replacement.

## 10. Marketing Strategy

### Content pillars

1. **"How it's made"** — storytelling about craft, materials, heritage. Why a Goodyear-welted sole matters. How wax cotton is made. The history of Barbour.

## 17. Taxes & Accounting

### Registration

1. **Below €2,200/year turnover:** No KvK registration required, no VAT
2. **Above €2,200/year:** Register as eenmanszaak (sole trader) at KvK — **€82.25** one-time fee
3. **Below €20,000/year turnover:** Can opt for KOR (Kleine Ondernemersregeling) — VAT-exempt, simpler admin. But you can't reclaim input VAT.
4. **Above €20,000/year:** Standard VAT registration required

**At your current pace (~€100/month revenue), you're at ~€1,200/year — below the threshold. But if you scale to target, register early.** Registration gives you credibility and allows you to use the margin scheme properly.

### The Margeregeling (Margin Scheme) — your best friend

When you buy from private individuals (not businesses), you only pay 21% BTW on your **profit margin**, not the full sale price.

**Example:**
- Buy jacket on Vinted for €30 (from a private seller)
- Sell on Grailed for €150
- Margin = €120
- BTW owed = 21% of €120 = €25.20
- Effective tax rate on revenue: 16.8%

**Two calculation methods:**
1. **Per-item method:** Calculate margin per item. Simple, transparent.
2. **Globalisation method:** Pool all buys and sells per period. Losses offset gains. Better if you have some items that sell below purchase price.

### Bookkeeping requirements

- Record purchase price, description, and selling price for each item
- Keep margin goods separate from non-margin goods
- Retain all records for 7 years
- File quarterly BTW returns (or annual if on KOR)

### Tools

- **MoneyMonk** (€14/month) — Dutch accounting software, good for sole traders
- **Tellow** (free tier) — simpler, auto-imports bank transactions
- **Boekhoud Gemak** — another Dutch option
- **Spreadsheet** — honestly fine at this stage. Track: item name, purchase price, platform fees, shipping cost, sell price, profit.

### Practical recommendation

1. Register as eenmanszaak at KvK now (€82.25)
2. Start with KOR exemption (no VAT charging/reclaiming) — simpler
3. Use a spreadsheet or Tellow to track every buy and sell
4. When turnover approaches €20,000, switch to full VAT + margin scheme
5. Consider a boekhouder (accountant) for your annual tax return — costs ~€300-500/year for a simple eenmanszaak

### Customs considerations for Japan/US buyers

- **Japan:** Import duty on clothing ~10%, consumption tax 10%. Buyers are used to this.

- **Always declare accurate value** — don't undervalue for customs, it's illegal and risks seizure
